What is this tool?

This tool performs a counterpoint analysis on the musical "voices" within a MIDI file. A MIDI file contains information about the musical notes played at specific times. It specifies when each note occurs throughout a piece of music. Although MIDI is not an audio file format, you can play MIDI files on a computer and on many electronic keyboards.

What is counterpoint?

In music theory, "counterpoint" refers to the analysis of how musical "voices," or distinct parts or lines, interact with and relate to each other. For example, how does a trumpet part relate to a trombone part, or how does a violin part relate to a cello part? When the violin notes go up, do the cello notes go down? These are the kinds of questions that counterpoint analysis seeks to answer.

What are the types of counterpoint?

  1. Similar
  2. Parallel
  3. Oblique
  4. Contrary

When two voices play at the same time, they can move in various directions relative to each other. The four types of contrapuntal motion are similar, parallel, oblique, and contrary. If they both move in the same direction, they are said to have "similar" motion. If they both move in the same direction with the same distance (or "interval"), they have "parallel" motion. If one voice remains the same while the other moves up or down, the motion between the two voices is "oblique." And if the two voices move in opposite directions, they exhibit "contrary" motion in that place.
