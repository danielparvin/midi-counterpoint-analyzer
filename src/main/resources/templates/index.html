This tool performs a counterpoint analysis on the musical "voices" within a MIDI file. A MIDI file contains information about the musical notes played at specific times. For example, it might specify that a certain Bb occurs at such and such time in a piece of music, and a certain F# occurs at other specific times. Although MIDI is not an audio file format, MIDI files represent when specific notes should be played. People can play MIDI files on a computer and on many electronic keyboards.

In music theory, "counterpoint" refers to the analysis of how musical "voices," or distinct parts or lines, interact with and relate to each other. For example, how does a trumpet part relate to a trombone part, how does a violin part relate to a cello part, or how does a soprano line relate to an alto line? These are the kinds of questions that counterpoint analysis seeks to answer.

When two voices play at the same time, they can move in various directions relative to each other. The four types of contrapuntal motion are similar, parallel, oblique, and contrary. If they both move in the same direction, they are said to have "similar" motion. If they both move in the same direction with the same distance (or "interval"), they have "parallel" motion. If one voice remains the same while the other moves up or down, the motion between the two voices is "oblique." And if the two voices move in opposite directions, they exhibit "contrary" motion in that place.
